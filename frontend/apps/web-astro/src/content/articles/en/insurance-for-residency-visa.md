If you are applying for residency in Spain, one of the required documents is **medical insurance**. But not just any policy will do — consulates and immigration authorities have specific requirements. Let's break them down in plain words.

## Which policy a consulate will accept

To get your residency application approved, the medical policy must meet four conditions:

- **Full coverage (sin copagos)** — no co-payments for visits or procedures. This is the key point: policies with co-payments are usually not accepted for a visa.
- **No waiting periods** — coverage works from day one, not after several months.
- **For the full year** — issued for at least 12 months and valid across all of Spain.
- **With a consulate certificate** — a separate document (usually a PDF) that explicitly confirms the policy meets residency requirements.

## What the consulate actually checks

The consulate officer doesn't look at a glossy brochure — they read the wording in the certificate: does it mention full coverage with no co-payments, no waiting periods, and validity across Spain? Getting this document right is the single most common reason applications are delayed.

## What the policy covers

Beyond the visa requirements, a proper medical policy for residency usually includes:

- a primary-care doctor and 40+ specialties;
- 24/7 emergency care;
- tests, diagnostics and imaging;
- hospitalisation and outpatient surgery;
- basic dental.

So it's not a "piece of paper for the visa" — it's healthcare that actually works on Tenerife.

## Common mistakes

- **Buying travel insurance** — it won't be accepted for residency.
- **Saving on a policy with co-payments** — the application may be rejected.
- **Leaving it to the last minute** — better done early: a policy can be issued weeks before you apply.

> Tip: check in advance which visa type you're applying for (non-lucrative, digital nomad, family reunification) — the coverage details depend on it.
